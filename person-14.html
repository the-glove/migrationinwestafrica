El Hadji Bubacar Saidi et al.

El Hadji Bubacar Saidi et al.

El Hadji Bubacar Saidi lives in the town of Pirada in Guinea-Bissau, adjacent to the border with Senegal. During the Portuguese colonial period, the government was very difficult, requiring people to pay heavy taxes and participate in forced labor projects. During the war for independence he said, many joined the Portuguese side because they were scared of what would happen if you did not. El Hadji claimed that if you were in a Portuguese-held town and refused to fight for the Portuguese, they would hurt you or your family. He was forced into the Portuguese military during the war, where he served for six years alongside two of his friends (who were also forced). Villages were required to cook for Portuguese soldiers, and the war caused many to flee across the border into Senegal.

As a young man, he went to Senegal regularly to seasonally farm peanuts, and would purchase goods unavailable at home, like bicycles, and then return home. He would use the money to help his family pay colonial taxes.

Today, El Hadji is happy that Senegal and Guinea-Bissau are more closely tied today than before. Weekly border markets called lumo have increased connections between the two countries, as has increased cross-border marriage.

Interview

At that time, the elders would decide when we could go. We would farm our fields until they were done and then we could go to Senegal after the rainy season. We would go work harvesting peanuts for three or four months and then we could come back.

Did you ever do that?

We all did that. I did that. He did that. But the generation after us did not. If you did not have a wife yet, you could use that money for the dowry to get a wife. If you had a bad field, you could use that money to buy food. When you came back, you would not have wasted any of the money. When you first came back, even in the middle of the night, you would go to your father’s room, or your older sibling’s room, whoever was an elder, directly there. You would not dare bring it to your mother’s room.
